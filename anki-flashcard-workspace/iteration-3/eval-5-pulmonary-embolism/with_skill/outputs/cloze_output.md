| {{c1::Computed tomography pulmonary angiography (CTPA)}} is the first-line imaging modality for suspected acute pulmonary embolism. | 1 |
| A technically adequate CTPA study requires pulmonary trunk opacification to at least {{c1::250}} Hounsfield units. | 2 |
| Thrombus appears as a {{c1::filling defect}} within the opacified pulmonary artery lumen on CTPA. | 3 |
| Acute pulmonary emboli form {{c1::acute}} angles with the vessel wall, while chronic emboli form {{c2::obtuse}} angles. | 4 |
| An embolus at the main pulmonary artery bifurcation is described as a {{c1::saddle}} embolus in the sagittal plane. | 5 |
| Right ventricular enlargement on CT is defined by an RV:LV ratio greater than {{c1::1.0}} on reconstructed four-chamber views. | 6 |
| Right heart strain on CT shows bowing of the interventricular septum toward the {{c1::left ventricle}} and main pulmonary artery diameter greater than {{c2::29 mm}}. | 7 |
| Contrast {{c1::reflux}} into the inferior vena cava and hepatic veins on CTPA is a sign of right heart strain. | 8 |
| {{c1::Central}} pulmonary emboli (main, lobar) carry higher mortality than {{c2::peripheral}} emboli (segmental, subsegmental). | 9 |
| Chronic thromboembolic disease presents with {{c1::webs, bands, and intimal irregularity}} within the pulmonary arteries. | 10 |
| Chronic thromboembolic disease shows {{c1::mosaic attenuation}} on CT chest and evidence of pulmonary hypertension. | 11 |
| Distinguishing acute from chronic PE is critical because CTEPH patients may benefit from {{c1::pulmonary thromboendarterectomy (PEA)}}. | 12 |
