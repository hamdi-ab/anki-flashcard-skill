# Basic Cards — Pulmonary Embolism Imaging

| Front | Back | Number |
|---|---|---|
| What is the first-line imaging modality for suspected acute pulmonary embolism? | CT pulmonary angiography (CTPA) | 1 |
| What minimum opacification (in HU) is required for an adequate CTPA? | ≥250 Hounsfield units in the pulmonary trunk | 2 |
| How does acute thrombus appear on CTPA? | As a filling defect within the opacified pulmonary artery lumen | 3 |
| Where do acute emboli typically lodge? | At arterial bifurcation points | 4 |
| What angle do acute emboli form with the vessel wall? | Acute angles | 5 |
| What is a "saddle" embolus? | An embolus lodged at the main pulmonary artery bifurcation | 6 |
| What RV:LV ratio defines right ventricular enlargement on CT? | Greater than 1.0 on reconstructed four-chamber views | 7 |
| Name four CT signs of right heart strain from acute PE. | (1) RV enlargement (RV:LV >1.0), (2) septal bowing toward LV, (3) contrast reflux into IVC/hepatic veins, (4) main PA dilation >29 mm | 8 |
| Which emboli carry higher mortality — central or peripheral? | Central (main, lobar) emboli | 9 |
| What are the CT findings of chronic thromboembolic disease? | Webs, bands, intimal irregularity, mosaic attenuation, signs of pulmonary hypertension | 10 |
| What angle do chronic emboli form with the vessel wall? | Obtuse angles | 11 |
| What may be seen in chronic emboli that is not seen in acute? | Calcification | 12 |
| What surgical procedure benefits patients with CTEPH? | Pulmonary thromboendarterectomy (PEA) | 13 |
| Why is distinguishing chronic from acute PE pattern important? | Critical for surgical planning — CTEPH may benefit from PEA | 14 |
