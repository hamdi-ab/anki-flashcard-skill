Computed tomography pulmonary angiography (CTPA) is the first-line imaging modality for suspected acute pulmonary embolism. A technically adequate study requires opacification of the pulmonary trunk to at least 250 Hounsfield units. Thrombus appears as a filling defect within the opacified pulmonary artery lumen. Acute emboli typically lodge at arterial bifurcation points and form acute angles with the vessel wall. In the sagittal plane, acute emboli are often described as "saddle" when lodged at the main pulmonary artery bifurcation. Signs of right heart strain on CT include right ventricular enlargement (RV:LV ratio greater than 1.0 on reconstructed four-chamber views), bowing of the interventricular septum toward the left ventricle, reflux of contrast into the inferior vena cava and hepatic veins, and dilation of the main pulmonary artery greater than 29 mm. Central emboli (main, lobar) carry higher mortality than peripheral (segmental, subsegmental) emboli. Chronic thromboembolic disease presents differently: webs, bands, and intimal irregularity within the pulmonary arteries, often with mosaic attenuation on CT chest and evidence of pulmonary hypertension. Chronic emboli form obtuse angles with the vessel wall and may show calcification. Patients with chronic thromboembolic pulmonary hypertension (CTEPH) may benefit from pulmonary thromboendarterectomy (PEA), so identifying the chronic rather than acute pattern is critical for surgical planning.
